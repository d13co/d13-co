    Tinyman Governance: Community Audit

    <span title='2024-07-22 14:49:12.772 +0000 UTC'>July 22, 2024</span>&nbsp;·&nbsp;3 min&nbsp;·&nbsp;443 words&nbsp;·&nbsp;Bit @ D13.co | Suggest Changes

    When I initially started investigating and disclosing smart contract vulnerabilities, part of my intentions were to build up a portfolio in order to attract paid engagements as a smart contract auditor. This article discusses one such engagement, which came with a very interesting twist.

    Brief#

    The Tinyman team is developing an on-chain Governance platform for their protocol. With Tinyman being a staple of the Algorand DeFi ecosystem and a personal favorite of mine, when they approached me about my interest in auditing the contracts, the speech bubble over my head clearly read “you bet your tiny ass I’m in!”. As it turned out, it would be even more interesting that a simple solo audit: Tinyman put together an ad-hoc team of developers and experts in the AVM to review the contracts as a group.

    The team consisted of:

    • Nullun from Algorand Foundation
    • Gidon from Folks Finance
    • Bunsan from Vestige
    • Swaggelwander from Vestige
    • Kevin from Defly
    • Myself

    We audited the 4 smart contract that comprise the current iteration of the Tinyman Governance platform in a collaborative manner. Each team member could choose a particular focus, and we met on a weekly basis over the course of a few weeks in order to discuss findings and clarify questions. Fergal Walsh, who coordinated from the Tinyman end, made it clear that this was not intended to be a performative “tell me I’m pretty” audit, but that we should “tell it like it is” with regards to any areas we found lacking.

    Tealish#

    The contracts are written in Tealish, which my first contact with the language. I found it very straightforward to read and understand. It feels a bit like a typed python optimized for the AVM. Specifically, the combination of structs and box operations made abstracted away a some of the “data-layer” complexity.

    The only aspect that caught me off guard when working with struct properties was the immediate “save” when a new value was assigned to a struct property.

    @public()
func cancel_proposal(proposal_id: bytes):
    # ...
    box<Proposal> proposal = OpenBox(proposal_box_name)
    proposal.is_cancelled = BYTES_TRUE
    # ! saved immediately - no explicit box.save() required
    # ...
end

    To me, the object/ORM like “feel” of the pattern here creates the expectation that the changes would require a .save() or CloseBox() to be persisted to the box. As such, a couple of times during the audit I would squint at something think it is a no-op.
